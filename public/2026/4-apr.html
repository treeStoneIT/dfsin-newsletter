The Greatest Things

The late Earl Nightingale, our teacher, mentor and colleague, sent us some definitions one time called, "The Greatest Things." During our careers, we've reviewed them often to help us remember just what's important and what isn't.

Here they are - The Greatest Things:

  • The best day, today.
  • The greatest puzzle, life.
  • The best policy, honesty.
  • The greatest thought, God.
  • The greatest mystery, death.
  • The best work, work you like.
  • The greatest mistake, giving up.
  • The most ridiculous asset, pride.
  • The greatest need, common sense.
  • The most dangerous person, a liar.
  • The best advice, use good manners.
  • The wisest short-cut, develop mentors.
  • The greatest fault, to be aware of none.
  • The greatest truth, we reap what we sow.
  • The most expensive indulgence, self-pity.
  • The greatest deceiver, one who deceives self.
  • The best habit, making good on all commitments.
  • The best teacher, one who brings out the best in you.
  • The saddest feeling, feeling envious of another's success.
  • The greatest thing in the world, love - love of family, home, friends, associates, company and country.

And here are some definitions taken from the backs of business cards, meeting notes and emails accumulated along the way. They are from some of the top salespeople in the world.

  • The greatest handicap, egotism.
  • The greatest victory, victory over self.
  • The most certain thing in business, change.
  • The greatest job, being needed and appreciated.
  • The greatest gamble, substituting hope for facts.
  • The strongest competitive edge, a high level of energy.
  • The most effective selling habit, sound time management.
  • The greatest guarantee of success, honest, intelligent effort.
  • The best action, keeping the mind clear and judgment good.
  • The greatest selling strategy, speaking as one who has authority.
  • The greatest waste, the vast reservoir of talents and abilities most of us possess but never quite get around to using.

Good luck and good selling.

Ted Talk

Having just returned from a Leadership and Management Practices conference in Nashville, I was fascinated by all the AI tools and forward thinking that field leadership is using across the globe. A lot of these will be presented to advisors as your businesses evolve. I imagine a time where AI can make some of your more arduous tasks easier, your more awkward phone calls nicer, and your ability to envision something for your business and AI makes it a reality for you.

In other news. LIMRA's 2025 results were just finalized. It appears that most of the insurance growth came from direct channels, which is disheartening. At DFSIN we better resemble the growth in the MGA/Career channels and while there was some growth in that area, it's not as significant as the growth in direct to consumer sales. Just something to be aware of. Direct to consumer is transactional, something like term insurance can be easily sold this way, but the advice world (where you are) needs to show its value in the insurance space.

Check out the highlights of growth in 2025. UL had strong growth, so did whole life. This is the area that you're most valued as these types of sales are unlikely to be sold through a website.

Compliance and Important Information

Security

A big part of information security involves being vigilant and adopting good habits. The loss of an unencrypted USB key or hard drive containing client information, the theft of a laptop left on the back seat of a car, and the accidental disclosure of a password to a fraudster are all examples of events that could generate major monetary losses and, even more importantly, shake client confidence in you and the organization.

Given the above, we're asking you to take cybersecurity training. For more information please log into WEBI and go to Rep Services Technology.
