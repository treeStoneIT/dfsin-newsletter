Sales Ideas to Propel Your Summer Sales Performance

  1. Remind yourself, selling is a numbers game.
  2. Look at clients as if they were prospects.
  3. Examine the type of market you've successfully worked.
  4. Examine your product mix. You may be discussing "X" when your prospect wants "Y."
  5. Show a million dollar proposal to someone each week.
  6. Have five client review interviews each week.
  7. Contact one "over-sixty" prospect each day.
  8. Implement marketing strategies, i.e., socials, direct mail, joint selling.
  9. Use these words with prospects twice each day, "Now, here's what I'd like to recommend ..."
  10. Spend time with centers of influence.
  11. Look back to sales concepts that worked for you in the past.
  12. Spend time with a mentor.

Tips & Information from Erin Meisner

What Happens After We Submit Your Application?

Here's what to expect once we hit "submit" — so nothing catches you off guard.

The timeline. The insurance company will begin reviewing your application within days or receipt. Most decisions come back well before this, but insurers reserve up to 90 days to complete their review and sometimes it can even take longer due to delays outside of our control.

They may ask follow-up questions. As the underwriter reviews your information, they might need clarification or additional details. If that happens, I'll reach out and walk you through exactly what's needed — sometimes it's a short questionnaire, sometimes a quick conversation.

Medical requirements can come later. Even if the insurer initially says no medical work is needed, that can change as they dig deeper. They may request blood work, a brief exam, or other health information after your application is already in process. This is normal.

Your doctor may have to complete a report. Insurers often request an Attending Physician Statement (APS) — essentially a summary of your medical records from your doctor's office. We don't control how quickly your doctor responds, so this step can sometimes stretch the timeline.

The possible outcomes. Once the review is complete, the insurer will do one of the following:

  • Approve your policy exactly as applied for
  • Approve your policy with an adjusted premium based on their risk assessment
  • Approve with exclusions on specific conditions
  • Postpone a decision to a future date
  • Decline the application

If they adjust the premium or add exclusions, the final terms may differ from the initial pricing we reviewed.

What I'll do throughout. I'll keep you updated at every step. You won't have to wonder where things stand — when I hear something, you'll hear something.

The best outcome is a quick approval exactly as applied for. But if the process takes a turn, you'll already know it's a normal part of how underwriting works — and I'll be right here to guide you through it.

Your next step: If I need anything from you, I'll reach out directly — and we'll work through it as quickly as possible.

Why attach a cover letter?

A well-written cover letter is a strategic tool that explains the context and purpose of the insurance application. It helps us understand the client's needs and make an informed decision.

It also serves to:

  • Introduce the client and provide essential information that isn't included in the application
  • Speed up processing by minimizing the need for added communications between us, the client and you
  • Facilitate a more comprehensive assessment by highlighting key facts
